Trace sizing

Trace Width & Current Capacity

A copper trace has resistance. Pass too much current through a trace that's too narrow and it heats up — at worst it acts as a fuse and blows, destroying the board. Calculating correct trace width is a non-negotiable design step for any power-carrying trace.

The IPC-2221 formula

The standard formula for calculating the maximum current a trace can carry is defined in IPC-2221. It depends on the allowed temperature rise above ambient, the trace cross-sectional area, and whether the trace is on an outer layer (exposed to air) or an internal layer (trapped in substrate).

IPC-2221 outer-layer approximation:
I = k × ΔT⁰·⁴⁴ × A⁰·⁷²⁵
k = 0.048 (outer layer) · 0.024 (inner layer)
ΔT = temperature rise (°C above ambient)
A = trace cross-section in mils² = width(mil) × thickness(mil)
// In practice: use Saturn PCB Toolkit or KiCad's built-in calculator

You don't need to solve this by hand — free calculators do it for you. But understanding what the variables mean helps you make better decisions: a 10°C temperature rise allowance gives a much narrower trace than a 30°C allowance. Internal traces carry roughly half the current of outer layer traces at the same width because heat can't escape as easily.

Always add margin

The IPC-2221 figures are maximums, not design targets. Design for at least 1.5–2× the expected current. A trace running near its limit gets warm, accelerates oxidation, and can fail under surge conditions. If in doubt, go wider — a wider trace never hurts.

Copper weight and its effect

Copper weight describes how thick the copper layer is. "1 oz copper" means 1 oz of copper per square foot of board — which works out to approximately 35 micrometres (35µm) thick. This is the standard for most PCBs.

0.5 oz

~17.5µm

Used for very fine-pitch PCBs and flex circuits. Half the current capacity of 1oz at the same width. Not standard — only specify if needed.

1 oz — standard

~35µm

The default. Suitable for all signal traces and most power traces up to 2–3A. All trace width calculators default to this. Use this unless you have a reason not to.

2 oz

~70µm

Doubles current capacity at the same trace width. Used in power electronics, motor drivers, battery chargers. Typically costs more and can cause minimum trace/space issues at fine pitch.

Connecting layers

Via Types in Depth

A via is more than just a hole. The type of via — through-hole, blind, buried, or microvia — determines manufacturing cost, design density, and which layers it can connect.

Via types on a 4-layer board — through-hole, blind, buried, and microvia

Through-hole vias

Drilled through the entire board and plated. Connect any two layers. The only type available on standard 2-layer boards and the cheapest to manufacture. Minimum drill diameter at hobbyist fabs: 0.3mm (some offer 0.2mm). The pad around the hole is called the annular ring — keep it at least 0.15mm wide for fab reliability.

Blind vias

Start at an outer layer and stop at an inner layer — visible from one side only. Require separate drilling processes per depth, significantly increasing manufacturing cost. Used in high-density designs (smartphones, BGA-heavy boards) where through-holes would waste space on inner layers. Not available at standard hobbyist fab prices.

Buried vias

Connect only inner layers — invisible from both outer surfaces. Must be drilled and plated before the outer layers are laminated on. Most expensive via type. Used in ultra-dense multilayer designs. Requires careful design planning because they're committed before the board is complete.

Microvias (HDI)

Laser-drilled blind vias, typically 0.1mm or smaller diameter. Used in High Density Interconnect (HDI) boards — the technology that makes modern smartphone mainboards possible. Not mechanically drilled. Filled with conductive or non-conductive fill. Dramatically increases routing density. Available at JLCPCB and PCBWay at extra cost for HDI stackups.

Beyond copper

Non-Electrical PCB Layers

Your Gerber export contains more than just copper layers. Understanding what each non-electrical layer does helps you use them correctly in your designs.

Layer

Courtyard (F.Courtyard / B.Courtyard)

A rectangle drawn around every component showing its "keep-out" area — the minimum space it needs. KiCad uses courtyard layer overlap as a DRC error to flag components placed too close together. The courtyard includes the component body, pads, and a small clearance margin (typically 0.25–0.5mm). Does not appear on manufactured boards — it's a design tool only.

Layer

Fabrication (F.Fab / B.Fab)

Shows a simplified outline of each component's physical body — the actual shape of the IC package, connector housing, etc. Used in assembly drawings and manufacturing documentation. More accurate than silkscreen for showing what the assembled board actually looks like. Not printed on the board itself.

Layer

Edge Cuts (Edge.Cuts)

Defines the physical boundary of the board — where the routing machine cuts. Must be a completely closed shape. Any copper or silkscreen that extends into the Edge.Cuts boundary is trimmed by the router, potentially exposing copper at the board edge. Keep copper at least 0.25mm inside the Edge.Cuts boundary.

Layer

User Drawing Layers

KiCad provides User.1 through User.9 layers for documentation: board dimensions, mechanical notes, zone annotations, or assembly instructions. None of these appear on the physical board. Use them freely for design notes — they're purely for your reference and for collaborators reading the design file.

Layer

Solder Paste (F.Paste / B.Paste)

Defines the apertures in the solder paste stencil — the openings through which paste is applied during SMD assembly. By default, the paste layer matches the pad shape. For thermal pads on QFN packages, the paste aperture is often reduced (60–80% of the pad area) to prevent bridging. If you're doing hand soldering only, the paste layer is irrelevant.

File

Drill File (Excellon)

Not technically a Gerber layer but a separate file format specifying every drill hit: position, diameter, and whether it's plated (PTH) or non-plated (NPTH). Non-plated holes are used for mechanical mounting (screw holes without electrical connection). Always generate and include the drill file with your Gerber submission — without it, the factory cannot drill your board.

Materials

Board Materials

FR4 is not the only substrate. Different applications call for different materials.

MaterialDescriptionTypical useCost
FR4
Flame Retardant 4		 Woven fibreglass + epoxy resin. Standard material for 99% of boards. Rigid, durable, inexpensive. Everything: hobbyist boards, consumer electronics, industrial Baseline
Rogers RO4003C
RF laminate		 Controlled dielectric constant (3.55 ± 0.05), very low signal loss at microwave frequencies. Not suitable for soldering with standard FR4 profile. RF amplifiers, antennas, 5G infrastructure, radar 10–20× FR4
Polyimide (Kapton)
Flex PCB base		 Flexible film substrate that survives repeated bending cycles. Can be combined with rigid sections (rigid-flex boards). Wearables, foldable phones, hinge mechanisms, drones 3–8× FR4
Metal core (MCPCB)
Aluminium backed		 Aluminium base with a thin dielectric layer then copper. Excellent thermal conductivity — heat from components flows directly into the metal base. High-power LEDs, motor drivers, power supplies 2–4× FR4
CEM-1 / CEM-3
Composite epoxy		 Paper or non-woven fibreglass with epoxy. Cheaper than FR4 but lower mechanical strength and higher moisture absorption. Simple consumer electronics, cost-sensitive single-layer boards 0.5–0.8× FR4

Beginner material choice

Use FR4. Always. For every board you make in the next several years, FR4 is correct. The exotic materials are for very specific applications and add significant design complexity. Order standard 1.6mm FR4 from any fab house and you'll never be wrong.
