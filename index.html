The basics

What is a PCB?

A Printed Circuit Board is a flat board that does two things simultaneously: it mechanically holds your electronic components in place, and it electrically connects them — without a single wire.

Instead of wire, the connections are thin copper pathways chemically etched into the board's surface. These are called traces. Combined with holes (vias) that connect layers, and pads where components solder on, the board becomes a complete, reusable wiring system.

The word "printed" is historical — it comes from the printing-like photographic process used to pattern the copper, not from a printer. The correct term is etched or patterned, but "printed" stuck.

  • →Mechanical support — holds every component at a precise, repeatable position
  • →Electrical connections — copper traces replace physical wires entirely
  • →Repeatability — a thousand identical boards can be manufactured from one design file
  • →Miniaturisation — components can be packed far more tightly than point-to-point wiring allows

Before PCBs

Point-to-point wiring

Every connection was a physical wire soldered between components. Unreliable, slow to build, impossible to mass-produce, and prone to vibration failure.

The PCB revolution

Etched copper connections

Copper traces replace wires entirely. The same design can be mass-produced with identical connections every time — enabling everything from radios to iPhones.

Today

Accessible to everyone

Free EDA software, $2 prototyping services, and worldwide shipping mean anyone with a laptop can design a board this week and hold it in their hands next week.
