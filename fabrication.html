01substrate

Substrate preparation

Large sheets of FR4 come pre-laminated with copper foil — typically 35µm (1 oz/ft²) on one or both sides. Sheets are cut to panel size. Multiple boards are manufactured on a single panel for efficiency; your 5 boards from JLCPCB will all be on the same panel, separated and broken apart at the end.

02photoresist

Photoresist coating & UV exposure

A light-sensitive chemical (photoresist) is applied to the copper surface. The board is then exposed to UV light through a photomask — a film printed from your copper Gerber file, where black areas block UV and clear areas let it pass. UV-exposed areas harden; unexposed areas wash away in developer solution, leaving bare copper wherever copper should be removed.

03etching

Chemical etching

The board enters a bath of ferric chloride or ammonium persulphate — an acid that dissolves copper. Everywhere the photoresist washed away, the acid attacks the copper, removing it. Where hardened photoresist remains, copper is protected. Result: your traces and pads exactly as drawn. The resist is then stripped off.

04drilling

CNC drilling

A CNC machine drills all holes from your Excellon drill file — through-hole component holes and via holes — using carbide drill bits. Modern machines drill thousands of holes per minute with ±0.05mm positional accuracy. The drill file you generated during Gerber export controls every hole diameter and location.

05plating

Electroless copper plating

Drilled holes are coated with copper via an electroless (chemical) plating process, then thickened by electroplating. This deposits a conductive copper barrel inside each via hole, electrically connecting the copper layers through the hole. Without this step, a via would just be an empty tunnel with no connection between layers.

06soldermask

Solder mask application

Liquid epoxy solder mask is applied to the whole board surface and photo-developed — the same process as the copper layer. It is opened at pad locations (solder mask openings) and cured. The colour you choose (green, red, blue, black, white, yellow) is simply the colour of this epoxy. It protects copper from oxidation and prevents solder from bridging between adjacent pads during assembly.

07silkscreen

Silkscreen printing

White (or occasionally yellow or black) ink is inkjet- or screen-printed onto the solder mask, from your silkscreen Gerber file. This shows component designators (R1, U2, C4), polarity markers (+, K, pin 1 dots), and any text you added. It has absolutely zero electrical function — purely for human readability during assembly, testing, and repair.

08finish + QC

Surface finish, singulation & electrical testing

A surface finish is applied to all exposed copper pads (see below). The panel is then routed or scored to separate individual boards. Finally, automated electrical testing (flying probe or bed-of-nails test fixtures) verifies no short circuits or open connections exist. Boards that fail testing are scrapped before shipping.

Pad protection

Surface Finishes

Bare copper oxidises within hours. A surface finish is applied to every exposed pad to prevent oxidation and ensure good solder wetting during assembly.

HASL (Lead-free)

Baseline cost — included free at JLCPCB

Hot Air Solder Levelling. The board is dipped in molten solder then levelled with hot-air knives. Creates a tin-silver-copper coating on all pads. Cheap, robust, and very solderable. Slightly uneven surface — not ideal for fine-pitch SMD (0.5mm pitch QFP/QFN) but perfect for 0805 components and through-hole.

ENIG

~£1–2 extra per board at JLCPCB

Electroless Nickel Immersion Gold. A nickel barrier layer then a very thin gold flash over all pads. Perfectly flat surface — excellent for fine-pitch ICs and BGA pads. Gold prevents oxidation indefinitely. The better choice once you start using QFP, QFN, or any SMD with pitch below 0.8mm.

OSP

Usually cheapest or free

Organic Solderability Preservative. A thin organic coating protecting copper until soldering. Flat surface like ENIG. Disadvantage: limited shelf life (6–12 months), and unsoldered pads start oxidising after first reflow. Not ideal for boards you'll rework multiple times or store for later.

ENEPIG

Premium — 3–5× HASL cost

Electroless Nickel Electroless Palladium Immersion Gold. Nickel + palladium + gold. Wire-bondable surface, long shelf life, excellent reliability. Used in automotive and aerospace applications. Complete overkill for hobbyist boards — file it away for future reference.

Which finish should you choose?

Start with HASL lead-free — it's free and works for everything with 0805 or larger SMD, or through-hole. Once you use fine-pitch QFP or QFN packages, upgrade to ENIG for its flat, reliable surface. The extra cost is negligible when you're already paying for shipping.

Tip: panel multiple copies

JLCPCB charges by panel area, not board count within that area. If your board is small (under 5×5cm), you can tile multiple copies on one panel using V-score or mouse-bite breakaway tabs between them — getting 10 or 20 copies for the same price as 5. Search "KiCad panelisation" for tools that automate this.

Going further

PCB Assembly (PCBA) Services

Once you're comfortable designing boards, PCBA services let you outsource the soldering too. You upload your Gerbers, a Bill of Materials (BOM) listing every component, and a component placement file (CPL). The factory sources the components (from LCSC for JLCPCB), applies solder paste, places components with pick-and-place machines, and runs the boards through a reflow oven.

For SMD-heavy boards with many tiny components, PCBA is often actually cheaper than buying all the components separately and soldering by hand — especially at quantities of 5–50 boards. JLCPCB charges from $8 setup plus $0.0017 per SMD component placed.

PCBA component availability

Not all components are in LCSC's library. When designing for PCBA, check that your BOM components are available on LCSC (lcsc.com) before finalising your schematic — or you'll need to hand-solder the unavailable ones after the PCBA run. In KiCad, you can add LCSC part numbers directly to your schematic symbols for easy BOM generation.
