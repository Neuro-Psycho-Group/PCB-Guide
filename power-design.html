Power Supply Design

The most common source of first-board failures. How to select the right regulator topology, size your capacitors correctly, calculate thermal dissipation, and handle USB power safely.

Why power matters most

Power Is the Foundation

Everything on your board depends on clean, stable power. A noisy supply corrupts ADC readings. An underrated regulator resets under load. A poorly chosen capacitor causes a switching supply to oscillate. Insufficient current causes voltage rail sag that manifests as mysterious glitches in completely unrelated parts of the circuit.

Beginners often treat the power supply as an afterthought — grab a common LDO, slap a couple of capacitors on it, and move on. This section teaches you to treat power design as a first-class design concern, not a checkbox.

The two things power design always requires

No matter what regulator you choose: (1) Input and output capacitors sized for the regulator's datasheet requirements — not just any capacitor, but the correct type and value. (2) Decoupling capacitors on every IC power pin — placed as close as possible to the pin. These two things, done correctly, solve 80% of power integrity problems before they start.

Choosing a topology

Regulator Topologies Compared

The fundamental choice in power design: how much input-output voltage difference do you have, and how much current do you need? These two factors drive the topology decision.

Linear regulator
LDO Regulator

The simplest regulator: passes current through a transistor in a controlled way, burning the excess voltage as heat. "LDO" stands for Low Drop-Out — the minimum voltage difference between input and output at which it still regulates. Extremely simple to implement: input cap, regulator, output cap. That's it.

Switching regulator (step-down)
Buck Converter

Rapidly switches a transistor on and off, using an inductor and capacitor to smooth the result into a regulated DC output. Highly efficient because the transistor is either fully on (low resistance, low loss) or fully off (no current, no loss) — it's never in a linear region burning power as heat.

When to use an LDO vs a buck converter

Use an LDO if: your input-output voltage difference is small (less than 1–2V), your current requirement is low (under 300–500mA), or you need the cleanest possible supply for analog circuits (ADC, RF). Use a buck converter if: your input voltage is significantly higher than output (e.g. 12V in, 3.3V out), your current requirement is above 500mA, or battery life matters. The crossover point is roughly where the LDO's heat dissipation becomes a problem: P_loss = (Vin − Vout) × Iload. If that product exceeds about 0.5–1W, a buck converter saves you from a heatsink.

The simple one first

LDO Regulator Design

An LDO regulator in its simplest form needs only two capacitors — one at the input, one at the output. But the capacitor type and value matter more than most beginners realise.

Minimal LDO circuit: input cap, regulator, output cap, GND

The capacitor type problem

Many LDO datasheets specify that the output capacitor must have a minimum ESR (Equivalent Series Resistance) — typically 0.1–1Ω. Ceramic capacitors have extremely low ESR (~1–10mΩ), which actually makes some older LDOs oscillate. Always check the datasheet: if it specifies a minimum ESR, use a tantalum or low-ESR electrolytic. Modern LDOs (AP2112, MCP1700, TLV755) are designed for ceramic capacitors and are more forgiving.

Thermal calculation for LDOs

An LDO dissipates heat equal to the voltage drop multiplied by the current: P = (Vin − Vout) × Iload. This is the power you must get out of the package. If it can't dissipate fast enough, the LDO hits its thermal shutdown temperature (usually 125–150°C) and shuts off.

// Example: AMS1117-3.3, Vin=5V, Iload=500mA

More power, less heat

Buck Converter Design Fundamentals

A buck converter is more complex than an LDO but follows a well-defined design process. The key components to size are: the inductor, the output capacitor, and the feedback resistors.

Core design equations

L = (Vin − Vout) × Vout / (ΔIL × fsw × Vin)

L = inductor value (H) · ΔIL = peak-to-peak ripple current (typically 0.2–0.4 × Iout_max)

fsw = switching frequency (Hz) · Vin = input voltage · Vout = output voltage

// Example: Vin=12V, Vout=3.3V, Iout=1A, fsw=500kHz, ΔIL=0.3A

L = (12−3.3)×3.3 / (0.3×500000×12) = 28.71 / 1,800,000 ≈ 16µH → use 15µH or 22µH

Output voltage setting (adjustable buck)

Vout = Vref × (1 + R1/R2)

Vref = reference voltage (from datasheet, typically 0.8V or 1.25V)

R1 = top feedback resistor (between Vout and FB pin)

R2 = bottom feedback resistor (between FB pin and GND)

// For TPS62125 (Vref=0.5V): to set Vout=3.3V, use R2=100kΩ → R1=(3.3/0.5−1)×100k = 560kΩ

The inductor — the component beginners get wrong most

The inductor is often treated as "just pick any value near the datasheet recommendation." It's not. Three specs matter:

  • Inductance value (µH) — calculated from the design equation above. Using a value that's too small increases current ripple (more output noise, higher peak currents). Too large increases transient response time.
  • Saturation current (Isat) — the current at which the core material saturates and inductance drops dramatically. Your inductor's Isat must exceed your maximum load current plus half the ripple: Isat > Iout_max + ΔIL/2. A saturated inductor in a switching supply usually means immediate failure.
  • DCR (DC winding resistance) — the resistance of the copper winding. Lower is better — it's a direct power loss. For a 1A supply, even 200mΩ DCR wastes 200mW as heat in the inductor itself.

Never design the peak inductor current near Isat. The cliff is sudden — a saturated inductor acts almost like a short circuit, destroying the FET.

Buck converter PCB layout rules

Switching supplies are far more layout-sensitive than linear regulators. The switching node (where the inductor connects to the switch) carries large, fast current transients that radiate if the copper area is large.

  • Keep the switching node copper area small — connect IC switch pin → inductor → output capacitor with short, direct traces. Don't flood this node with copper.
  • Input and output capacitors close to the IC — place them immediately adjacent to the IC input and output pins. The high-frequency current loop is: input cap → switch → inductor → output cap → back to input cap. Keep this loop as small as possible.
  • Feedback trace away from the switching node — the feedback trace (from Vout to the FB pin via the resistor divider) senses the output voltage. Route it away from the inductor and switching node to avoid noise pickup. It should be a short, direct trace.
  • Solid ground plane under the IC — the IC's exposed thermal pad (if present) must connect to a ground plane for heat dissipation. Add multiple vias under the thermal pad.

The bootstrap capacitor

Most synchronous buck converters have a BST (bootstrap) pin that requires a small capacitor (typically 100nF) between BST and SW (switching node). This is different from the decoupling capacitors on VIN and VOUT. Forgetting it usually means the converter doesn't start or has poor high-side gate drive. Always check the "recommended application circuit" in the datasheet — it shows every required capacitor.

The universal input

USB Power Input Design

USB is the most common power source for hobbyist and consumer electronics. But USB has evolved considerably — what works for USB 2.0 chargers may not negotiate correctly with USB-C Power Delivery. Here's what you actually need to know.

USB 2.0 · Standard downstream port

USB 2.0 (Type-A, Micro-B, Mini-B)

Provides 5V VBUS at up to 500mA by default — no negotiation required for this level. To draw more than 100mA (the default if no enumeration occurs), USB 2.0 technically requires enumeration. In practice, most USB chargers provide 500mA or more without enumeration. Safe default assumption: 500mA from any standard USB port.

USB Charging · BC 1.2

Dedicated charging ports

USB Battery Charging Spec 1.2 allows dedicated charger ports to provide up to 1.5A. Detection: the charger shorts D+ and D−. Your device detects this short and can request up to 1.5A. Requires detection circuitry in your device, or simply connect D+ to D− via a 200Ω resistor to signal "I want to charge at 1.5A" to compatible chargers.

USB-C · Default current

USB-C without Power Delivery

A USB-C port at default current can provide 5V at 900mA (USB 3.x) or 500mA (USB 2.0). Critical requirement: the CC1 and CC2 pins on a USB-C connector must each have a 5.1kΩ pull-down resistor to GND. Without these, a USB-C charger detects no device attached and provides no power. This is the single most-missed detail in USB-C designs.

USB PD · Power Delivery

USB-C Power Delivery

Negotiates voltages beyond 5V (9V, 12V, 15V, 20V) and currents up to 5A (100W). Requires a dedicated USB PD controller IC (FUSB302, STUSB4500, CH224K) to handle the CC communication protocol. Without PD negotiation, a PD-capable charger falls back to 5V standard. The CH224K is a simple, cheap PD sink controller popular for quick "I want 9V or 12V from this USB-C charger" designs.

Essential protection for USB power input

USB-C power-only design: 5.1kΩ pull-downs on CC1 and CC2 are mandatory — without them the charger sees no device

Any external connection is a potential source of damage. USB VBUS in particular can carry voltage spikes (from hot-plugging) and reverse polarity (from non-standard cables or faulty chargers).

Protection

Polarity protection

A Schottky diode in series with VBUS (anode to VBUS, cathode to your power rail) blocks reverse polarity. Voltage drop: ~0.2–0.3V. Alternatively, a P-channel MOSFET ideal diode circuit has near-zero voltage drop and is preferred for battery-powered designs where every millivolt matters.

Protection

ESD protection

A TVS (Transient Voltage Suppressor) diode across VBUS clamps voltage spikes from ESD events. Use a TVS with a clamping voltage slightly above 5V (e.g. 5.5V working voltage, 9V clamping voltage). PRTR5V0U2X is a common choice that protects both VBUS and the data lines simultaneously.

Protection

Overcurrent protection

A self-resetting polyfuse (PTC resettable fuse) in series with VBUS limits current to a safe level and resets when the fault clears. Choose a PTC with a hold current above your normal draw and a trip current below the USB port's limit. MF-MSMF series from Bourns is commonly used.

Filtering

Input EMI filter

A ferrite bead (or common-mode choke for USB data) in series with VBUS, combined with a bulk capacitor (100µF), filters conducted emissions from the USB cable and prevents your switching supply from injecting noise back onto the USB bus. Required for CE/FCC compliance on commercial products.

What goes wrong

Common Power Design Mistakes

Using the wrong capacitor type for a switching regulator

Mistake: placing a 100µF aluminium electrolytic as the output cap of a buck converter running at 500kHz+

Electrolytic capacitors have high ESL (equivalent series inductance) and are ineffective at high frequencies. The output capacitor of a switching supply needs to handle ripple current at the switching frequency — use ceramic X5R or X7R capacitors for this. The datasheet will specify the required capacitance and ESR. Use multiple smaller ceramics in parallel rather than one large electrolytic.

Under-sizing the inductor saturation current

Mistake: choosing an inductor with an Isat rating equal to the average load current

Inductor current has ripple — it's not flat DC. The peak current reaches Iavg + ΔIL/2. If the inductor's Isat is less than this peak value, it saturates, its inductance drops to near zero, and the converter sees almost a direct short, sending massive current through the switching FET. Choose Isat > 1.3 × Iout_max as a conservative margin.

Missing CC pull-downs on USB-C

Mistake: designing a USB-C power-only connector without 5.1kΩ resistors on CC1 and CC2

The board simply won't power up from USB-C chargers. This is the single most common USB-C mistake. The CC pins are how a USB-C charger knows a device is connected. Without 5.1kΩ to GND on both CC1 and CC2, the charger detects an open circuit and doesn't enable VBUS. Two resistors, 5.1kΩ each, CC1→GND and CC2→GND. Always. No exceptions for power-only USB-C.

LDO input capacitor too small

Mistake: using only a 100nF capacitor on the LDO input

The input capacitor on an LDO supplies current during fast load transients — the time before the upstream supply can respond. 100nF is the decoupling capacitor for high-frequency noise; the bulk input capacitor should typically be 10µF ceramic (or 100µF electrolytic) to prevent voltage droop when the load suddenly steps up. Many LDO datasheets specify minimum input capacitance — check it.

No bulk capacitance at the board power entry point

Mistake: relying entirely on per-IC decoupling without a large bulk capacitor near the power input connector

When a motor, relay, or large digital load suddenly draws current, the wire/trace impedance from the power supply causes a voltage dip. A large bulk capacitor (typically 100–470µF electrolytic) right at the power entry point acts as a local reservoir, supplying that initial surge current before the external supply can respond. Especially important for motor drivers, H-bridges, and anything with large inrush current.

Ceramic capacitor derating — capacitance that isn't there

Mistake: specifying a 10µF X5R MLCC capacitor rated 10V and using it at 5V — and expecting 10µF

Ceramic (MLCC) capacitors exhibit significant capacitance loss with applied DC voltage — a property called DC bias derating. An X5R 10µF 10V capacitor may only provide 5–7µF at 5V DC. X7R and C0G have better (though not perfect) DC bias behaviour. For critical applications: check the capacitance-vs-voltage graph in the datasheet. Use a larger-rated capacitor than you need, or use more capacitors in parallel.

X5R dielectric loses 40–60% capacitance at half its voltage rating. C0G (NP0) is stable across voltage. Always check the datasheet derating curve.
