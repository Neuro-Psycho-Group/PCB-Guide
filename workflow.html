Schematic craft

Schematic Best Practices

A schematic is a communication document — it needs to be readable by someone other than you, six months from now. These conventions are the difference between a professional schematic and a confusing tangle.

Reference designators

Every component on your schematic gets a reference designator (refdes) — a letter prefix followed by a number. These are printed on the silkscreen too, so they link schematic to physical board.

Net naming

Name your power nets meaningfully. Instead of just "VCC" everywhere, use +3V3, +5V, VBAT, USB_VBUS. Name important signal nets too: SPI_CLK, UART_TX, nRESET. This makes the schematic self-documenting and the PCB layout — where these net names appear when you hover over traces — much easier to navigate.

The n or / prefix conventionally means active-low: nRESET or /CS means the signal is active (does its thing) when pulled low. This is a widely-understood shorthand that communicates signal polarity at a glance.

Schematic layout conventions

  • Signal flow left to right — inputs on the left, outputs on the right
  • Power flows top to bottom — positive supply at the top, GND at the bottom
  • Group by function — power supply circuitry together, MCU with its supporting components, each peripheral in its own area
  • Never use 4-way junctions — a "T" junction (three wires meeting) is clear; a "+" junction (four wires crossing and connecting) is ambiguous. Use two separate T-junctions
  • Avoid long wires crossing everything — use net labels instead. A label "SPI_CLK" on a pin is clearer than a wire travelling across three blocks of components
  • Add pin numbers to every component — helps correlate schematic pins to real IC pinouts during debugging
  • Add a title block — board name, revision (start at Rev 1.0), date, and your name. When you have five versions of the same board, you'll thank yourself

Net labels replace long wires · VCC at top, GND at bottom · T-junctions not + junctions

Decoupling caps — where to put them in the schematic

Place each decoupling capacitor immediately adjacent to the IC it serves, with its own explicit VCC and GND connections. Don't string decoupling caps on a single long net that connects to all ICs — they should each have a direct, short, obvious connection to their IC's power pin. This also guides your PCB placement correctly.

ERC errors you'll always see as a beginner

ERC errorWhat it meansHow to fix
"Pin unconnected" A component pin with no wire or net attached to it Connect it, or add a "no connect" flag (X symbol in KiCad) if the pin is intentionally unused
"Power pin not driven" A power rail (like GND or VCC) has no power source Add a PWR_FLAG symbol to the net — this tells ERC "yes, this net is a power source"
"Wire not connected" A dangling wire end that connects to nothing Delete the wire or connect its loose end to a pin or junction
"Conflicting net names" Two different net labels are physically connected Remove one label or ensure the nets should actually be the same

Version control your schematics

Keep your KiCad project in a Git repository from day one. KiCad's file formats are text-based, which means git diffs are readable and meaningful. When you make a mistake on revision 3 that worked in revision 1, being able to git diff and see exactly what changed is invaluable. GitHub offers free private repos.

Phase 1 — Design

Schematic Capture

1

Before touching the EDA tool

Define your requirements

Write down what the board needs to do before opening any software. What's the supply voltage? Which ICs are you using? What connects to the outside world? A simple block diagram on paper saves hours of redesign later.

  • List every external connection: power input, outputs, communication interfaces
  • Download datasheets for every IC you'll use before starting the schematic
  • Note the power topology — regulated 3.3V? 5V from USB? Battery + charger?
  • Decide on form factor — does the board need to fit in a specific enclosure?
2

The most critical step

Schematic capture

The schematic is the logical truth of your circuit — every component and every electrical connection, but not physical positions. Think of it as a circuit diagram with real component symbols. Never open the PCB layout editor until your schematic is complete and correct. Any error here means rework after layout, which is far more painful.

  • Use net labels (named wires) instead of drawing long wires across the page
  • Add all power pins explicitly — missing a VCC pin on an IC is a very common beginner mistake
  • Place 100nF decoupling capacitors on every IC power pin in the schematic now
  • Label everything: values, reference designators, voltage rail names
  • Run the ERC (Electrical Rules Check) before leaving the schematic editor

What is a net?

A net is a named electrical node — every pin labelled the same name is connected. In layout, the EDA tool shows thin ratsnest lines between all pads that share a net, telling you which pads need a copper connection.

Phase 2 — Layout

Placement & Routing

3

Before routing

Assign footprints

Every schematic symbol must link to a physical footprint — the pad pattern the component sits on. If your footprint is wrong, the component won't fit when you try to assemble the board.

  • Always verify your chosen footprint against the component's datasheet land pattern
  • Use established library footprints where possible (KiCad's built-in libraries are trustworthy)
  • For unusual components, measure the datasheet dimensions carefully and double-check
4

80% of the work

Component placement

Placement determines how easy or hard routing will be. Good placement means short, direct traces. Poor placement means crossed traces and layer changes everywhere. Spend real time on placement before drawing a single trace.

Group power supply, MCU, and outputs separately. Place decoupling caps physically touching their IC. Connectors at the board edge. Short ratsnest lines = good placement.

  • Group by function: power circuitry together, digital logic together, analog separated from digital
  • Place decoupling capacitors immediately adjacent to their IC's power pins
  • Connectors go at the board edge — never buried in the middle
  • Orient ICs so their signal pins face the components they connect to
  • Leave routing channels — don't pack components so tight there's no room for traces
  • Consider mechanical constraints: mounting holes, case walls, connector clearances
5

Connect the dots

Trace routing

Now you draw copper traces to connect all nets, guided by the ratsnest lines. Route the most critical signals first: power rails, clocks, high-speed data lines.

  • Power traces: 0.5mm minimum, wider for higher current (see table below)
  • Signal traces: 0.2–0.3mm for typical logic at 3.3V/5V
  • Use 45° bends, not 90° corners (cleaner manufacturing, better signal integrity)
  • Add a ground plane — flood fill the bottom copper with GND polygon
  • USB D+/D− must be routed as equal-length, tightly coupled differential pairs

Logic signals (3.3V / 5V)0.15–0.25mmStandard digital I/O
Power (up to 1A)0.5mmAdd margin; err wider
Power (1–3A)1.0mmWide pour or polygon fill

6

Catch errors before ordering

Design Rule Check (DRC)

The DRC automatically scans your layout against manufacturing constraints. It flags traces too close together, pads without connections, copper outside the board outline, and much more. Run the DRC. Fix every error. Run it again.

  • Download your fab house's design rules file and import it into KiCad before running DRC
  • JLCPCB common minimums: 0.1mm trace width, 0.1mm clearance, 0.2mm drill diameter
  • Errors must be fixed; warnings should be investigated and understood
  • Also run the Footprint vs Netlist check to confirm every pad is on the correct net
Phase 3 — Fabrication

Gerbers & Ordering

7

Export manufacturing files

Generate Gerber files & order

Gerber files are the universal manufacturing format for PCBs. Each layer is a separate file. Bundle them into a ZIP and upload to your fab house.

Always preview your Gerbers first

Open your Gerber files in a viewer (KiCad's built-in viewer or JLCPCB's online Gerber viewer) before uploading. Verify every layer looks correct. A misexported Gerber is invisible until the board arrives wrong.

Phase 4 — Assembly

Solder & Test

8

Physical build & bring-up

Solder, power on, and test

Once your bare boards arrive, solder all components. Then before applying power: visually inspect under good light for solder bridges. Measure resistance between VCC and GND — should not be near zero (indicates a short).

  • Use a current-limited bench supply for first power-on — limits damage if there's a short
  • Check power rail voltages first, then clock signals, then communication buses
  • UART debug serial output is your best friend — add serial prints to firmware early
  • Probe test points (TP1, TP2…) with a multimeter or oscilloscope

Pre-Order Checklist

Run through these before uploading Gerbers:

  • Schematic ERC passes with zero errors
  • All footprints verified against datasheets
  • All nets routed — no remaining ratsnest lines
  • DRC passes with zero errors using fab house rules
  • Ground plane poured and stitched
  • Board outline on Edge.Cuts, closed contour
  • Silkscreen text not overlapping pads
  • Gerber files previewed in a viewer
  • Drill file included in the ZIP
  • Ordered 5 boards minimum (cost is nearly the same as 1)
