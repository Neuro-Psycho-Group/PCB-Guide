06 / Soldering

Iron in Hand

The equipment you need, the technique that works, how to identify and fix bad joints, and staying safe. Soldering is a learnable skill — everyone starts badly.

The material

Solder: What It Is and What to Buy

Solder is a fusible metal alloy that melts at low temperatures to create a permanent, electrically conductive join between a component and a PCB pad.

Solder joint anatomy — cross-section comparison of good and common defective joints

Modern solder is a tin/silver/copper alloy (SAC305: 96.5% Sn, 3% Ag, 0.5% Cu) for lead-free, or traditionally tin/lead (Sn63Pb37: 63% Sn, 37% Pb). Good solder joint appearance is shiny and concave — a properly wetted, smooth fillet between the pad and the component lead.

Solder wire contains a flux core — a chemical that activates on heating to clean oxidation from the surfaces being joined, allowing the solder to flow and wet properly. You cannot get a good joint without flux activity.

Lead-free vs leaded solder

Leaded solder (Sn63Pb37, melts at ~183°C) is easier to work with — it flows better, wets more readily, and the joints are slightly shinier. Lead-free (SAC305, melts at ~217°C) requires higher temperatures and more precise technique, but is safer and RoHS compliant. Beginners: start with leaded solder in a well-ventilated area. Wash hands after every session.

Sn63Pb37

Solder Paste

Tiny balls of solder suspended in flux gel. Applied to SMD pads via stencil or syringe. Required for reflow soldering SMD components.

For reflow / SMD work

Wire diameter

Buy 0.6mm or 0.7mm diameter solder wire for SMD work and general fine work. 1.0mm is fine for through-hole. Avoid anything thicker than 1.0mm for PCB work.

The toolkit

Equipment You Need

Essential

Temperature-controlled iron

The single most important investment. A station with a precise, adjustable temperature transforms soldering. Cheap un-regulated irons run too hot, damage components, and produce cold joints.

Hakko FX-888D · Weller WE1010 · TS100/TS101 (portable)

Essential

Soldering iron tips

Chisel tips (1.5–2mm) are the most useful all-rounders. Fine conical tips for tight SMD work. Keep tips tinned (coated in solder) when not in use to prevent oxidation.

Start with a chisel tip. Add a conical for SMD.

Essential

Flux

Additional flux beyond what's in the solder wire. Makes a dramatic difference to joint quality. Apply before soldering, especially for SMD drag-soldering and rework.

MG Chemicals 8341 no-clean flux pen · Amtech NC-559

Essential

Solder wick (desoldering braid)

Copper braid that, when pressed onto solder with a hot iron, wicks away the molten solder by capillary action. Essential for removing solder bridges and reworking mistakes.

Chemtronics Soder-Wick 80-3-5

Essential

Magnification

You cannot inspect SMD solder joints reliably with the naked eye. A 10× loupe or USB microscope reveals cold joints, bridges, and missing solder that are invisible otherwise.

$15 jeweller's loupe as minimum · Andonstar AD246 microscope

Strongly recommended

Hot air station

For SMD rework and soldering QFN/BGA packages. Blows hot air through a nozzle to melt SMD solder joints from above without touching the component.

Quick 861DW · Yihua 858D

Recommended

PCB vice / helping hands

Holds the board at a comfortable angle, freeing both hands for iron and solder. PCB vice with alligator clips or a dedicated PCB holder stand.

Kaisi K-800 · Panavise 301

Recommended

Isopropyl alcohol (IPA) + brush

99% IPA and a stiff brush (toothbrush) for cleaning flux residue after soldering. No-clean flux can be left, but cleaning always looks better and prevents long-term corrosion concerns.

99% IPA only — 70% leaves residue

Temperature settings

Iron Temperature Guide

Temperature matters enormously. Too cool: solder won't flow properly, joints are grainy. Too hot: you damage components, lift pads, and oxidise the tip instantly.

Technique

Through-Hole Soldering: Step by Step

The most important technique rule: always heat the joint, then feed solder to the joint — never melt solder on the iron tip

01

Insert and secure the component

Push the component through from the top. Bend the leads slightly outward (about 15–20°) on the underside — this holds the component in place while you flip the board to solder.

02

Heat the joint — pad AND lead together

Place the iron tip so it touches both the copper pad and the component lead simultaneously. Hold for 2–3 seconds to bring both to temperature. You are heating the joint, not the solder.

03

Apply solder to the joint (not the iron)

Touch the solder wire to the junction of the pad and lead — not to the tip of the iron. When the joint is hot enough, the solder will flow instantly into the joint. Feed in enough for a good fillet, then stop.

04

Remove iron, don't move the joint

Remove the iron (and solder wire first) then the iron. Do not move the component or the board for 3–5 seconds while the solder solidifies. Movement during solidification causes a "cold joint" — grainy appearance, poor contact.

05

Inspect and clip

A good through-hole joint looks like a small smooth volcano — shiny, concave fillet that coats the lead and wets into the pad. Use flush cutters to clip the lead close (but not flush) to the solder. Then inspect under magnification.

Technique

SMD Soldering: Iron & Hot Air

Surface mount soldering is different from through-hole in every way — the scale, the tools, the order of operations, and the failure modes. Here's how to do it properly with both an iron and hot air.

An iron works well for SMD components down to 0402 size, SOIC-8/16, and with care even TQFP packages. The technique is different from through-hole — you pre-tin one pad, tack the component, then solder the remaining joints.

SMD iron technique: flux first → pre-tin one pad → place with tweezers → tack with iron → solder other pad → reflow first joint

01

Add flux to the pads

Apply a small amount of flux to the pad area. This is not optional for SMD — it makes the difference between solder that flows perfectly and solder that balls up. A flux pen is easiest; a syringe of gel flux also works.

02

Pre-tin one pad

Apply a small amount of solder to just one pad — the one you'll use to tack the component. The goal is a thin, flat layer of solder on the pad, not a blob. This is your anchor point.

03

Place and tack

Using tweezers, position the component on its pads. Hold it in place with the tweezers, then briefly touch the iron to the pre-tinned pad — the solder reflows and tacks the component's first contact. Now you can release the tweezers. The component is held by one joint; check alignment carefully before proceeding.

04

Solder remaining contacts

For a two-pad component (resistor, capacitor): solder the other pad normally. Apply a fresh dab of flux, touch the iron to the junction of the pad and the component contact, and feed in a small amount of solder. For multi-pin ICs (SOIC etc.): solder the opposite corner pin first to anchor both ends, then work around the remaining pins.

05

Go back and reflow the tack joint

The very first joint (your tack) has less solder than the others. Go back, add flux and a tiny amount of solder, and reflow it to match. Then inspect every joint under magnification.

Drag soldering for ICs

For SOIC, TQFP, or other multi-pin ICs, drag soldering is faster than soldering each pin individually. Apply generous flux to all the pins, load a small amount of solder onto a chisel tip, and drag it along the row of pins in one smooth motion. Surface tension pulls the solder to each pin. Any bridges are removed with fresh flux and wick. This sounds scary but is genuinely faster and cleaner than pin-by-pin once you practise.

Hot air is ideal for removing and replacing SMD components, soldering QFN packages with thermal pads, and any rework that an iron can't reach cleanly. The component floats on molten solder paste — surface tension self-aligns it when everything is properly applied.

01

Apply solder paste or flux

For new placement: apply solder paste to each pad with a syringe (or a mini stencil). For rework of an existing joint: apply liquid flux. The paste amount should roughly match the pad area — thin and even, not a thick blob.

02

Place the component

Position with tweezers. Approximate alignment is fine — the component self-aligns once solder melts (for small passives and ICs up to QFP). For large BGAs, placement must be accurate as self-alignment is weaker at that scale.

03

Set hot air station temperature and airflow

For leaded solder paste: 300–320°C, medium airflow. For lead-free: 350–380°C. Lower airflow prevents blowing components off pads. Use the correct nozzle — a flat or narrow nozzle for fine work, a wide nozzle for large components.

04

Heat evenly in circular motions

Hold the nozzle 15–25mm above the component and move in slow circles. Don't focus on one point — heat the entire component and pads together. Watch for the solder paste: first it bubbles (flux boiling off), then goes grey and lumpy, then suddenly becomes bright and liquid. When it transitions to liquid, all joints are reflowed. Remove the air immediately.

05

Don't move the board — let it cool

The solder solidifies within 5–10 seconds of removing the heat. Don't touch anything. Once solid, inspect under magnification. Adjacent components can be displaced by airflow — check that nothing nearby moved during reflow.

Reflow soldering is the industrial method — and fully achievable at home with a cheap toaster oven or dedicated reflow oven. It's far faster than iron soldering when you have many SMD components, and gives more consistent results.

The reflow process

01

Apply solder paste via stencil

A stainless steel stencil (cut to match your solder mask openings — JLCPCB can supply one) is placed over the board and held in position. Solder paste is spread across it with a squeegee, depositing paste onto every pad through the stencil apertures. The stencil is then lifted away. Result: perfectly sized paste deposits on every pad in one pass.

02

Pick and place all components

Place each component onto its paste deposits using tweezers. The paste is sticky and holds components in position. Work methodically across the board. Approximate alignment is fine — components self-align significantly during reflow. Do not jostle the board after placement.

03

Run the reflow profile

A reflow profile has four stages: preheat (ramp slowly to ~150°C, driving off paste solvents), soak (hold 150–180°C for 60–90s, activating flux), reflow (ramp quickly above solder melting point, ~220°C for SAC305), and cool (ramp back down). A dedicated reflow oven follows this profile automatically. A toaster oven requires manual temperature monitoring with a thermocouple.

04

Inspect and clean

After cooling, inspect every joint under magnification. Check for bridges (common where pins are close), tombstoned components, and insufficient paste. Clean flux residue with 99% IPA and a brush. Fix any defects with an iron and additional flux.

Beginner reflow setup

A cheap T962 reflow oven (~£50–80 on eBay) is a reasonable entry point. It works but has hot spots — search for "T962 firmware upgrade" for a community fix. A better alternative is a hotplate (like the MHP30) used as a bottom-side preheat, combined with hot air for reflow. Dedicated PCB reflow ovens (Controleo3, Whizoo Clapotis) are excellent but cost £150–300.

Quality control

Identifying and Fixing Bad Joints

Every soldering beginner makes these. Learn to recognise them — a bad joint that looks fine visually is how a board fails two weeks later.

Cold joint

Cause: joint not hot enough when solder applied

Looks dull, grainy, or crystalline — like dried mud rather than a smooth shiny surface. The solder didn't properly wet and bond to both surfaces. High resistance, can fail intermittently.

Fix: Apply flux, reheat the joint fully, let solder reflow. Remove excess with wick if needed, then resolder properly.

Solder bridge

Cause: too much solder, pads too close

An unintended blob of solder connecting two pads or traces that should not be connected. Creates a short circuit. Common on fine-pitch SMD components.

Fix: Apply flux (very important), press solder wick onto the bridge with the iron. The wick absorbs the excess solder. Inspect carefully after.

Lifted pad

Cause: too much heat, too long, or mechanical stress

The copper pad separates from the PCB substrate. Usually irreversible. The trace connecting to it may also tear. Common when desoldering without enough flux or when pulling a THT lead sideways.

Fix: Carefully scrape the trace to expose bare copper, solder a short wire jumper to the trace end, connect to the component pad. Prevention is better than cure.

Insufficient solder

Cause: not enough solder applied

Too little solder to form a proper fillet. The pad may be visible around the lead with little coverage. The joint may have adequate conductivity initially but is mechanically fragile.

Fix: Reheat, add a small amount of additional solder to build up the fillet properly. Aim for a smooth concave volcano shape.

Tombstoning (SMD)

Cause: one pad reflows before the other

A small SMD component (resistor, capacitor) lifts one end off its pad and stands vertically — like a tombstone. Happens in reflow when surface tension pulls the component up. One leg connected, one floating.

Fix: Use tweezers to press the component flat while reheating with hot air. Prevention: ensure both pads heat simultaneously. Check paste volume on both pads.

Solder ball

Cause: solder paste moisture, or solder spatter

Tiny balls of solder scattered around the board, not attached to any joint. Can roll around and cause intermittent short circuits. Common with contaminated paste or when solder spatters during THT work.

Fix: Inspect carefully under magnification. Remove with sticky flux brush or IPA clean. Prevent: store paste in fridge, avoid moisture in paste.

Dewetting

Cause: contaminated surface, old solder, no flux

Solder appears to have pulled back from the surface, leaving exposed copper surrounded by a ring of solder. The solder refused to bond to the base metal. Looks like the solder is repelled.

Fix: Clean with IPA, apply fresh flux, reheat. May need to remove old solder completely and redo from scratch.

Component shifted (SMD)

Cause: airflow during reflow, placement error

Component has moved from its intended position — rotated, offset from pads, or completely off the pads. One leg on, one partially on. Can cause intermittent contact or short.

Fix: While solder is still hot (or reflowing with hot air), gently nudge component back to correct position with tweezers. Work quickly.

Safety

Soldering Safety

Flux fumes

Flux fumes are respiratory irritants. Solder in a well-ventilated room, or better, use a fume extractor (a fan + activated carbon filter held near the soldering point). Never solder in an enclosed space. This is the most important safety rule.

Burns

Soldering irons run at 320–400°C. Burns are easy, heal slowly, and are entirely avoidable. Put the iron back in its stand immediately when not in use. Never hold a board with your hand while soldering it. Set up proper work lighting so you can see clearly without leaning in close.

Lead exposure

If using leaded solder: wash hands thoroughly after every soldering session. Don't eat while soldering. Keep children away from the workspace. Dispose of solder offcuts appropriately. Lead in solder is a different level of risk to, say, lead in paint — but basic hygiene is still important.

Component disposal

Electronic components contain various heavy metals. Don't throw them in household waste. Most areas have WEEE (Waste Electrical and Electronic Equipment) recycling facilities. Supermarkets often have small electronics drop-off bins.

ESD precautions

Static electricity can silently destroy CMOS components. Use an anti-static wrist strap when handling bare ICs and PCBs, especially MOSFET-input devices and microcontrollers. Affordable wrist straps are available for £2–5.
